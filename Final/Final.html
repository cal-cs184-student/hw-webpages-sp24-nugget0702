Abstract

An exploration of physics based graphics via Fluid Simulations. Fluids is a rich topic in physics and condensing its physical properties to encode realistic fluid rendering is something that our team finds fascinating to explore. In this project, we explored fluid dynamics like pressure and density, as well as marching cubes for simulations. This was a very fulfilling experience for the team as we read and implemented some papers referenced below and worked with Unity as our simulator.


Technical Approach

Particle Object and Spawning:

Our fluid simulation begins with the definition and creation of a particle object, which serves as the basic unit for which our fluid mesh is built off of. These particles' positions and behavior are calculated and adjusted throughout the simulation to mimic realistic fluid dynamics. We begin by spawning these particle objects into a 3D grid, systematically populating a predefined bounding box with particles at calculated intervals.


Density Calculation:

After introducing these particles into the system we use a custom density calculation function to approximate the local density around each particle by considering the presence of neighboring particles within a defined radius. The function calculates density by integrating the squared inverse distance of all particles within this radius. This emphasizes the influence of nearer particles more heavily, effectively capturing how crowded a particle's immediate surroundings are.


Pressure Gradient Calculation:

The following step involves determining the direction of the gradient of this density field. By leveraging the calculated density we are able to determine the direction of the gradient of this density field essentially indicating the direction of highest increase in particle density. We do this with the calculation of the pressure gradient. This process begins with a helper function designed to estimate the pressure at a point between two particles based on their densities relative to a target density. We utilize a tunable pressure multiplier parameter in order to scale how responsive the fluid is to changes in density relative to the target, resulting in the following equation for each particle: particle_pressure = (particle_denisty - target_denisty) • pressure_multiplier. The average of pressures from two neighboring particles is then calculated to get a scalar value representing the mutual pressure exerted between the two interacting particles. In order to calculate the full pressure gradient, the gradient vector for a given particle is calculated by summing the contributions from all neighboring particles within the density radius. In order to retrieve only the necessary particles we use a GetGridPoints method which efficiently identifies only those particles within the neighboring grid cells that are likely to affect the current particle's pressure environment. For each particle, we get the normalized distance vector from the current particle to the neighboring particle to ensure that the calculation deals with directionality only. The magnitude of the pressure effect is scaled inversely with distance to allow particles closer to the current particle to have a stronger influence on the gradient calculation. This scaling factor strength is calculated as: (distance - density_radius) * 15 / (π • denisty_radius5). This factor is derived from the volume of a sphere with radius equal to the density radius. This ensures that the effect diminishes as particles are farther apart, up to the limit set by the density radius. The contribution of each nearby particle to the gradient vector at the current particle is calculated by multiplying the normalized direction vector by the calculated pressure and the scaling factor. This is then divided by the density of neighboring particles to normalize the influence each particle has based on its own density. This avoids denser particles from affecting the motion of less dense particles. All the individual contributions are summed giving the total pressure gradient vector for the current particle, which points in the direction of the greatest increase in pressure showing which direction the particle should move. This essentially simulates a fluid's flow going from high-pressure to low-pressure areas. Additionally, the influence of gravity is factored into the particle's motion, combining with the gradient to dictate how particles should move.


Rendering:

Rendering of the fluid is done by the Marching Cube algorithm that constructs the surface of the fluid as mesh. The algorithm dissects the scene/bounding box into smaller cubes with vertices called Grid Points or Voxels. Each grid point will carry a value representing the volume/density of the fluid at that position, thus creating a scalar field in the 3D space inside our bounding box. The value at a arbitrary location x in the space is calculated by (x) =jmjjW(x-xj ,h), where mj , j , xj refers to the mass, density, and position of particle j, W(x-xj ,h) refers to the smoothing kernel where h is the smoothing radius. We reference the exact formula of the kernel from a 2018 CS294 project by Utkarsh Singhal, Yiding Jiang, and Gauthier Dieppedalle.



The constructed mesh surface will then represent an approximated isosurface of the scalar field at a certain isolevel/threshold of our choice. The algorithm does this by marching through all small cubes, checking for vertices with values above the threshold, and we consider these vertices as “On”. Based on the arrangement of “On” vertices, we create a mesh at each cube to completely cut out those vertices. As there are 256 possible arrangements, we reference codes online to support the algorithm. The final generated mesh will be the combination of each individual mesh of the cube.




Optimization Techniques:

In order to optimize these computationally heavy calculations, we employ spatial partitioning to deal with the intensive task of evaluating interactions between every pair of particles. We use this spatial grid of cubes to hold every particle inside it so it only needs to look in the 27 surrounding cubes to calculate density, as opposed to the O(n2) runtime complexity that would've initially been used. While a KD-tree approach was considered to potentially optimize this further, it was found that the overhead involved actually resulted in slower computations compared to the spatial grid method. Another optimization involves normalizing the density calculation. By calculating both the integral and derivative of the weighting function used in the density calculation, changes to the density radius do not affect the results. Another optimization technique was to utilize Unity's capabilities for parallel processing. This allows multiple processes, such as the rendering and physics calculations, to be executed simultaneously, significantly speeding up the simulation process without sacrificing the detail and accuracy needed for high-quality fluid simulation.


Results

From our video, we have shown a fluid, initially grouped together as particles, drop down into a bounding box, where the fluids react and collide with each other and the cube that it is enclosed in. We also added a custom wave-generator, by moving one of the walls to induce and examine wave properties on the fluid. Given more time, we would like to implement shader properties for our fluid mesh, which is quite complex given the scope and timeline of our current project.
